Temperature readings at a number of weather stations in the Arctic reach 30 degrees Celsius above seasonal norms.
Was this just a one-off event? Perhaps not.

Research by the Arctic Assessment and Monitoring Programme (AMAP) suggests that between 1979 and 2019, average surface temperatures in the Arctic increased 3 times faster than the global average. How did we get to 30 degrees (and rising)?

The Arctic Sea Ice
Approximately 30° South of the North Pole the Arctic Circle defines the extent of the Arctic, the northernmost region of Earth.

The Arctic sea ice is frozen ocean water that increases and decreases in extent with the seasons.

However there has been a dramatic decline of the arctic sea ice extent in the past 30 years. Specifically, September 2012 marked a record minimum

Sea ice melting contributes to the acceleration of global warming since the large ice sheets are replaced by the dark ocean that absorbes more sunlight which further heats up the water.

The above visualisations illustrate how the global and local phenomena are interlinked:
The rising temperatures, cause glacial melting which in turn cause rising sea levels. With this project we aim to contribute towards raising awareness about climate change, and about the change already happening in the remote, but valuable region of the arctic.
The point of no return might still be uncertain, but we hope that we will never need to find out which one it was.
